Sample SBIR Technical Review

This review uses a fictional materials startup concept to demonstrate deliverable style and technical judgment. It is not agency-specific compliance advice and does not reflect any real client or NDA-covered work.

Proposal Snapshot

Fictional project: Scalable perovskite scintillator film for compact X-ray imaging
Agency stage: Phase I
Claim: Solution-processed scintillator films can achieve high light yield, low afterglow, and sub-100 µm resolution on a low-cost imaging panel.

Review Summary

The concept is plausible but currently under-scoped. The technical plan needs sharper go/no-go metrics, clearer film-thickness and process windows, stronger moisture/oxygen stability testing, and a more defensible comparison against commercial scintillators. The largest reviewer risk is that the proposal overclaims scalability without enough process-control evidence.

Strengths

  • Strong application need: lower-cost, high-resolution X-ray imaging remains a meaningful market and technical target.
  • Solution processing is a plausible cost advantage if film uniformity and stability are controlled.
  • Proposed optical characterization methods are directionally correct: PL, decay time, light yield proxy, and imaging resolution.
  • A Phase I can reasonably test feasibility if the team narrows the success criteria.

Main Technical Risks

RiskAreaIssueWhat is needed
High Film uniformity Proposal mentions “large-area coating” but does not define acceptable thickness variation, roughness, pinhole density, or batch-to-batch repeatability. Define uniformity targets and measurement method before fabrication begins.
High Stability Perovskite materials can degrade under moisture, oxygen, heat, and X-ray dose. Current plan lacks explicit accelerated aging and dose-exposure protocols. Add accelerated aging conditions, dose targets, and retained-performance thresholds.
Medium Resolution vs. light yield tradeoff Thicker films improve absorption but degrade spatial resolution through optical scatter and lateral light spread. Run a thickness sweep with both absorption and MTF/resolution metrics; report tradeoff explicitly.
Medium Benchmarking Comparison set should include commercial CsI:Tl, GOS, and relevant indirect/direct detector alternatives. Vague “better than conventional materials” claims will be penalized. Add a benchmark table with specific metrics and measurement conditions for each comparator.
Low Manufacturability Phase I should not promise manufacturing readiness, but it should define the coating method, substrate size, drying/annealing window, and defect inspection method. Add a process-control section scoped to Phase I feasibility, not production.

Recommended Phase I Milestones

Milestone 1: Formulation downselect

  • Screen 3–5 formulations
  • Metrics: PL intensity, emission wavelength, decay time, visible defects, shelf stability
  • Go/no-go: at least one formulation maintains >80% PL after a defined ambient or controlled aging test

Milestone 2: Thickness and process window

  • Coating thickness range: e.g., 25, 50, 100, 200 µm
  • Metrics: thickness uniformity, optical density, surface defects, adhesion
  • Go/no-go: coefficient of variation target and defect-density threshold defined before testing begins

Milestone 3: Imaging proof of concept

  • Test pattern imaging under defined X-ray beam conditions
  • Metrics: lp/mm, MTF if available, exposure condition, repeatability
  • Go/no-go: resolution target tied to film thickness and detector geometry

Milestone 4: Stability and dose exposure

  • Moisture/oxygen/thermal storage conditions
  • X-ray dose exposure condition
  • Metrics: PL retention, visual degradation, darkening, emission shift

Redline-Style Comments

“High light yield” should be replaced with a measurable target and method. If absolute light yield is not available, state the proxy measurement and the benchmark sample used.
“Scalable” should be limited to a Phase I coating-size claim. Reviewers will expect evidence for uniformity, yield, and process control at the stated scale.
“Low-cost” should be supported by a simple cost driver table: precursor cost, coating method, substrate compatibility, process temperature, encapsulation requirement.
“Stable” should be defined by time, environment, and retained performance. A stability claim without a protocol is not defensible.

Suggested Additions to Strengthen the Proposal

  • One risk table with mitigation strategy and fallback formulation for each identified risk
  • One benchmark table against CsI:Tl, GOS, and relevant direct-conversion detector alternatives
  • One schematic of the test stack and optical readout geometry
  • One milestone table with quantitative go/no-go criteria for each milestone
  • One commercialization paragraph explaining who would buy the detector and why incumbent materials are insufficient for that application

Suggested Deliverable to Client

For a real SBIR technical review engagement, I would return:

  • Annotated proposal with inline redline comments
  • Priority risk log (high, medium, low) with recommended resolutions
  • Milestone table with suggested go/no-go criteria
  • Benchmark gap analysis identifying missing comparators
  • Clear separation of technical recommendations from agency-specific strategy or scoring-criteria interpretation
Scope boundary: This sample is technical proposal review, not agency-specific compliance advice. Final proposal strategy should be aligned with the specific solicitation, review criteria, and program manager expectations.
